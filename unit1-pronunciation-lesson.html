UNIT 1 · PRONUNCIATION

Spanish sounds like it looks.

Once you learn a small set of sound rules, you can read almost any Spanish word out loud — even ones you've never seen before. Let's build that skill.

Key question

How do you know how to pronounce a Spanish word just by looking at it?

🎯 What you'll learn
  • The five Spanish vowel sounds — and why they never change
  • Consonants that sound different than they do in English
  • How to break any Spanish word into syllables and read it aloud
💡 Why it matters

Once you know the sounds, you're not memorizing words one by one anymore — you can read almost any Spanish word out loud, even ones you've never seen before.

Spanish is a phonetic language. 💬

That means every letter (or letter pair) makes the same sound, always — no matter what word it's in.

Compare that to English, where spelling is often a poor guide to pronunciation: think about how differently "though," "through," and "tough" end, even though they're spelled almost the same.

That's great news for you: it means pronunciation isn't something you memorize word by word. You learn the rules once, and they work everywhere.

Learn the sounds → read any word. That's the whole game in Unit 1.

The five vowel sounds 🔤

Spanish uses the same five vowel letters as English — a, e, i, o, u. But each Spanish vowel has exactly one sound, always.

English vowels, by contrast, can each stand for several sounds depending on the word — just look at how many ways "a" gets pronounced: cat, father, ape, about.

One more thing to listen for: Spanish vowels stay pure — they don't slide into another vowel sound the way English ones often do. Think of how "boat" actually ends closer to "bo-oht," or "day" trails into "day-ee." In Spanish, gato and mesa stay clean and flat the whole way through.

Consonants that trip up English speakers 🗣️

Most Spanish consonants sound close to their English versions. These are the ones worth learning on purpose:

h
Always silent. Never pronounced.
hola → "OH-lah"
j
Sounds like the English "h" — but often more guttural, produced further back in the throat. Closer to the "ch" in the Scottish "loch."
jugo → "HOO-go" (with a rougher h)
ll / y
Both sound like the English "y."*
llamar → "yah-MAR"
*A few regions vary (parts of Spain, Argentina/Uruguay) — no big deal, you'll pick up on it as you hear more Spanish.
ñ
Sounds like "ny," as in "canyon."
año → "AH-nyo"
r / rr
A single "r" is a quick tap; "rr" is rolled (sometimes called "trilled").
pero vs. perro
c
Hard "k" sound before a, o, u. Soft "s" sound before e, i.
casa vs. cielo
g
Hard before a, o, u — same as the English g in "go." Soft before e, i — sounds like the Spanish j: closer to the English "h," but often more guttural, produced further back in the throat.
gato vs. gente
b / v
Both make the same soft "b" sound. Spanish has no hard English "v."
vaca → "BAH-ca"
🎙️ Real Talk

Rolling your r's is a physical skill, not just a knowledge one — plenty of native speakers never fully master it either. If it's not coming naturally yet, start from the quick single tap (similar to the fast "dd" sound in the American English "ladder"), then build toward repeating it rather than forcing the full roll right away.

Special cases worth knowing ⭐

qu

Always makes a hard "k" sound — the u is silent. Queso sounds like "KEH-so."

z

Always sounds like an English "s" — never the buzzy English "z." Zapato sounds like "sah-PAH-toh."

x

Usually a "ks" sound, as in taxi. One notable exception: in words like México and Oaxaca, x sounds like "h" — a historical spelling holdover.

w

Rare in Spanish — mostly shows up in borrowed words like wifi or kiwi, pronounced close to the English "w."

Breaking any word into syllables

›

You don't need this to read Spanish out loud — the sound rules above already get you there. But once you're comfortable, learning to divide words into syllables yourself makes reading longer, unfamiliar words even easier. Here are the three rules.

1. Every syllable has exactly one vowel sound

A single vowel counts as one. Two vowels that blend together (a diphthong) also count as just one syllable.

cue counts as one syllable in es-cue-la, not cu-e-la
Diphthongs vs. hiatus: when two vowels split (and when they don't)

Vowels come in two types: "strong" (a, e, o) and "weak" (i, u). When a strong and weak vowel — or two weak vowels — sit next to each other, they usually blend into one syllable (a diphthong). When two strong vowels sit together, or when a written accent breaks up what would normally blend, they stay as separate syllables (hiatus).

gui-ta-rra — "ui" blends  ·  pa-ís — the accent forces a split  ·  con-ti-nú-a — same idea
Watch out for this mix-up: in que, the u is silent — qu together just makes a hard "k," so que is one clean syllable, "keh." In cue (like in escuela), the u is not silent — it blends with the e into a "weh" sound, so cue is "kweh." Same three letters, rearranged, with the u playing an opposite role each time.
2. A single consonant moves forward, to the next vowel

When one consonant sits between two vowels, it joins the syllable that comes after it.

ca-sa, not cas-a  ·  a-mi-go, not am-igo
3. Two consonants together usually split — with a few exceptions

Most consonant pairs divide, one to each syllable. But certain pairs are "unbreakable" and always stay together with the vowel that follows: bl, br, cl, cr, dr, fl, fr, gl, gr, pl, pr, tr — plus the letter pairs ch, ll, and rr, which act like a single letter.

One more thing: every word has a stressed syllable

When you say a Spanish word out loud, one syllable naturally gets a little more emphasis than the rest — that's the stressed syllable. A written accent mark (´), when you see one, is simply pointing at which syllable to stress. Not every word needs one. The full rules for exactly when a written accent is required are their own topic for a future unit — for now, it's just worth getting used to listening for which syllable carries the stress.

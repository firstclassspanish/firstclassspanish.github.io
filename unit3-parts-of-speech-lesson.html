UNIT 3 · PARTS OF SPEECH

Every word has a job.

Nouns, verbs, adjectives, and more — the categories that tell you what each word is doing in a sentence. The same categories work in English and Spanish.

What is a "part of speech"?

A part of speech (in Spanish, una parte de la oración) is just a category that tells you a word's job in a sentence — is it naming something, doing something, or describing something?

The classic count is eight: noun, pronoun, verb, adjective, adverb, preposition, conjunction, interjection. We're using nine, since articles work differently enough from regular adjectives to deserve their own category rather than being folded in. Either way, we're starting with the six that matter most for where you are right now — the rest are covered in Go Further below.

Every word you'll ever use fits one of these categories. Learn to spot them, and both Spanish and English grammar get a lot easier to talk about.

Six core categories to start

Noun
sustantivo (also called nombre)
Names a person, place, thing, or idea. Nombre literally means "name" — a handy way to remember what nouns do.
dog → perro
Pronoun
pronombre
Stands in for a noun. (Remember these from Unit 2!)
she → ella
Verb
verbo
Shows action, or a state of being.
runs → corre
Adjective
adjetivo
Describes a noun.
big → grande
Adverb
adverbio
Describes a verb — often how, when, or where.
fast → rápido
Article
artículo
Signals that a noun is coming.
the → el / la

Here's the actual rule: in English, descriptive adjectives usually come before the noun ("big dog"). In Spanish, they usually come after the noun ("perro grande"). Same categories, opposite default order — and it's consistent enough to count on.

Why this matters

Verbs are about to become the center of attention — Unit 4 is entirely about conjugating them. Knowing what a verb is, and being able to spot one in a sentence, is exactly what that unit builds on.

This also works in reverse: once you can name these categories in Spanish, you'll start noticing them in English too — most students find their English grammar gets sharper right alongside their Spanish.

The rest of the parts of speech

›

Between the six core categories above and these three, that's the traditional full set of parts of speech — not a deep dive on any of them, just enough to know they exist.

Prepositions

Show a relationship — often location or direction. English: in, on, with. Spanish: en, con, para.

Conjunctions

Connect words or ideas. English: and, but, or. Spanish: y, pero, o.

Interjections

Short bursts of emotion or reaction. English: wow, ouch. Spanish: ¡ay!, ¡olé!

Agreement is coming later

You may have noticed grande didn't change form in our example — but adjectives and articles in Spanish actually do change depending on the noun they describe. Articles alone have four forms: el (masculine singular), la (feminine singular), los (masculine plural), las (feminine plural) — all of them meaning "the." Which one you use depends on the noun that follows. The full rules, including adjectives, are their own topic, with their own unit, coming later.

Can adjectives ever go before the noun?

Yes — "after the noun" is the default, not an absolute rule. Some adjectives can move before the noun, and a few actually change meaning depending on where they sit. For example, un amigo viejo means a friend who is old (in age), while un viejo amigo means a longtime friend — same word, different placement, different shade of meaning. This is genuinely its own topic, best covered in a future unit focused specifically on adjectives — for now, just know that placement isn't as fixed as it might seem.

UNIT 6 · CONCORDANCIA (AGREEMENT)

Every article and adjective has to agree with its noun's gender and number.

In Unit 5, you learned every noun carries a gender and a number. Now you'll see why that mattered so much — articles and adjectives have to match whatever the noun is carrying, every single time.

Key question

Why does the whole sentence have to match?

🎯 What you'll learn

  • Why "everything agrees" is really one rule, not several separate ones
💡 Why it matters

Agreement is what makes Spanish sound right to a Spanish speaker's ear. Get the noun's gender and number down (Unit 5), and this unit is really just applying that same information outward.

One noun, three things that match it 🔗

In Unit 5, you learned every Spanish noun carries a gender (masculine or feminine) and a number (singular or plural). That information doesn't stay with the noun alone — it spreads to everything describing it.

Article — the little word before a noun ("the" or "a/an" in English). In Spanish, the article has to match the noun's gender and number.

Adjective — a word that describes a noun. In Spanish, adjectives also have to match the noun's gender and number.

Concordancia (agreement) — the rule that ties it all together: article, noun, and adjective all carry the same gender and number, matching each other like puzzle pieces.

Every article and adjective has to agree with its noun. One noun, three matching pieces.

Articles: definite and indefinite 🏷️

Spanish articles come in two types, same as English — and both types agree with the noun's gender and number.

Definite articles — English uses one word for this: "the" (the book, the books). Spanish uses four (el/la/los/las), depending on gender and number:

Singular Plural
Masculine el libro los libros
Feminine la mesa las mesas

Indefinite articles — English uses "a/an" for singular and "some" for plural. Spanish also uses four (un/una/unos/unas), following the same gender/number pattern:

Singular Plural
Masculine un libro unos libros
Feminine una mesa unas mesas
Same noun information (gender + number) you already know from Unit 5 — just picking which of eight articles matches it, definite or indefinite.

Adjectives agree too 🎨

Adjectives follow the same logic as articles — they shift their ending to match the noun's gender and number. Most adjectives ending in -o work like this:

Article
el
Noun
libro
Adjective
rojo
el libro rojo (the red book)

Now the opposite case — feminine and plural instead of masculine and singular:

Article
las
Noun
ventanas
Adjective
abiertas
las ventanas abiertas (the open windows)

Change the noun's gender, and the adjective's ending changes with it: la mesa roja (the red table). Change the number, and it changes again: los libros rojos, las mesas rojas.

One adjective, four possible endings — rojo, roja, rojos, rojas — depending entirely on what noun it's describing.

Go Further

Not every adjective follows the -o/-a pattern

›

The -o/-a adjective pattern is common, but plenty of adjectives work a little differently. Worth knowing about even before you've memorized them all.

Adjectives ending in -e

Some adjectives use the same form for masculine and feminine, only changing for number — inteligente (smart) stays inteligente for el libro inteligente and la mesa inteligente, but becomes inteligentes for the plural.

Adjectives ending in a consonant

Many of these also stay the same for masculine and feminine — fácil (easy) is el libro fácil and la mesa fácil — but add -es to go plural: fáciles.

Adjective position can shift meaning

Most descriptive adjectives come after the noun in Spanish (el libro rojo, not "el rojo libro"), but a handful change meaning depending on position — un amigo viejo (an elderly friend) vs. un viejo amigo (a longtime friend). Agreement rules stay the same either way; only the word order and shade of meaning shift.

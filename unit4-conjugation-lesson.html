UNIT 4 · CONJUGATION

One verb, six endings.

(though we'll only need five)

Every verb has multiple forms — conjugations — to express who's doing the action (person) at different times (tenses: present, past, future, and more). But every one of those forms traces back to the verb's base form, called the infinitive.

Key question

What is conjugation, and how do you do it in Spanish?

🎯 What you'll learn
  • What an infinitive is, and how to identify a verb's family (-ar, -er, -ir)
  • How to split a verb into its stem and ending
  • How to conjugate regular verbs to match any subject pronoun
💡 Why it matters

Conjugation is how Spanish shows who's doing the action — it's the one skill you'll use in literally every Spanish sentence you ever speak or write.

Two terms, one relationship 🔤

Before anything else, two terms are worth locking in — this whole unit is really just about the relationship between them. Once it clicks, it's easy.

Infinitive — a verb's name and base form. Not yet tied to any subject or time (tense) — just the plain "to ___" version (to speak, to eat, to live, etc.) you'd find in a dictionary entry. Hablar means "to speak." Comer means "to eat." Vivir means "to live."

Conjugation — what happens when you take that name and change its ending to match who's doing the action. Hablo, hablas, habla — all conjugated forms of the same verb, hablar.

Every verb has a name (its infinitive), and conjugation is just adjusting that name's ending to fit the subject. That's the entire relationship.

You already do this in English 🌉

Conjugation can sound like a uniquely Spanish thing. It isn't — English conjugates too, just far less dramatically. Take a regular verb like "to walk" alongside one of English's most irregular verbs, "to be":

to walk (regular) to be (irregular)
I walk am
you walk are
he/she/it walks is
we walk are
they walk are
Notice the difference: "to walk" barely changes at all — just one letter, and only for he/she/it. "To be" is highly irregular — nearly every form looks like a completely different word (am, is, are). Spanish sits at the other end of this spectrum: even its regular verbs conjugate for every subject, not just one. That's exactly why this unit exists.

One scope note before we go further: everything in this unit conjugates verbs into the present tense — action happening now. Spanish has other tenses too (past, future, and more), each with its own endings. Those come in future units; this one is your foundation.

Every infinitive has two parts ✂️

Break hablar apart and you get two pieces: the stem (la raíz) and the ending (la terminación). Throughout this lesson, the stem stays in blue and the ending stays in gold — so you can start training your eye to spot the split.

hablar
comer
vivir
Here's the actual process, spelled out:
  1. Start with the infinitive — hablar.
  2. Drop the ending (-ar, -er, or -ir), leaving just the stem — habl-.
  3. Add the ending that matches both the subject (which box in the Box of Six) and the tense. For yo in the present tense, that's -o: habl- + -o = hablo.

The three verb families 👪

Every Spanish infinitive ends one of three ways. That ending tells you the family — and every verb in a family conjugates the same way.

-AR
hablar (to speak)
caminar (to walk)
estudiar (to study)
-ER
comer (to eat)
beber (to drink)
aprender (to learn)
-IR
vivir (to live)
escribir (to write)
abrir (to open)

Conjugating hablar 📦

Here's the Box of Six from Unit 2 — now filled in with a real conjugated verb.

Singular Plural
1st person yo hablo nosotros hablamos
2nd person tú hablas — not used —
3rd person él/ella/usted habla ellos/ustedes hablan
Notice the pattern: each box gets its own ending. Learn where a subject sits in the Box of Six, and you already know which ending it needs.
Since the ending already tells you the subject, Spanish speakers often drop the pronoun entirely. Hablo already means "I speak" — you don't need to also say yo. Same for tú, nosotros, and ellos/ustedes. The one exception: 3rd person singular. Habla could mean he speaks, she speaks, or you (formal) speak — the ending alone can't tell you which. There, you'll usually want to keep the pronoun, use a name, or lean on context that's already clear from the conversation.

The same pattern, three families 🔁

Every regular verb in a family follows the exact same set of endings. Compare hablar, comer, and vivir side by side.

hablar comer vivir
yo hablo como vivo
tú hablas comes vives
él/ella/usted habla come vive
nosotros hablamos comemos vivimos
ellos/ustedes hablan comen viven
Once you've memorized one family's pattern, you can conjugate any regular verb in that family — not just the example. That's the whole payoff of this unit.

Go Further

Not every verb plays by these rules

›

Everything above covers regular verbs — the majority of what you'll encounter. A few categories bend the rules, worth knowing about even before you study them in depth.

Irregular verbs

Some verbs don't follow the family pattern at all — ser (to be) and ir (to go) are two of the most common. These get memorized individually rather than derived from a pattern.

Stem-changing verbs

Some verbs keep the regular endings but change a vowel in the middle — querer (to want) becomes quiero, not "quero." The ending pattern still applies; just the stem shifts.

Reflexive verbs

Some verbs describe an action done to oneself and need an extra pronoun — llamarse (to be called/named), as in me llamo... (my name is...). These have their own set of rules layered on top of regular conjugation.

This is just present tense

Everything in this unit covers the present tense — action happening now. Spanish has several other tenses (past, future, and more), each with its own conjugation patterns. Those are future units.

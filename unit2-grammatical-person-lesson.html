UNIT 2 · GRAMMATICAL PERSON

Every sentence has a "who."

Before verbs, before conjugation — every subject in a sentence falls into one of six categories. Once you can spot which one, everything else in Spanish grammar gets easier.

Two quick definitions first

Before diving into grammatical person, two terms are worth defining clearly, since we'll lean on both constantly from here on.

Subject — the person or thing doing the action in a sentence. In "Elena runs," Elena is the subject. In "We speak Spanish," we is the subject.

Pronoun — a small word that stands in for a name or noun, so you don't have to keep repeating it. Instead of saying "Elena runs. Elena is fast," you can say "Elena runs. She is fast." She is a pronoun standing in for Elena.

Every sentence has a subject — and that subject is often expressed with a pronoun instead of a repeated name. Keep both words in mind; they're about to come up a lot.

What is "grammatical person"?

Grammatical person is just a way of answering one question about any subject: who is it, relative to the person speaking? There are only three possible answers.

1st
The speaker
Me, or me and others.
I · we
2nd
The one spoken to
The person (or people) you're talking to.
you
3rd
Everyone else
Anyone or anything else being talked about.
he · she · it · they
Every subject you'll ever encounter — a name, a group, a pet, an object — fits into one of these three. That's the whole concept. Everything else in this unit is just learning to recognize it quickly.

Meet the Box of Six

Person tells you who. But subjects can also be singular (one) or plural (more than one) — so each of the three persons splits into two, giving six total boxes.

Let's start with English

English already sorts its pronouns into this same six-box grid — you just may never have seen it laid out this way before.

Singular Plural
1st I we
2nd you you
3rd he / she / it they
Notice something? English uses the exact same word — "you" — for both the singular and plural 2nd-person boxes. Spanish keeps these separate. That one difference explains most of what's different about the Spanish box below.
Rows = person

Moving down the rows tells you 1st, 2nd, or 3rd person — the same three categories from above.

Columns = number

Moving across the columns tells you singular (one) or plural (more than one).

Why this matters

Here's the payoff: in Spanish, the ending of every verb changes depending on which box its subject belongs to. That's why hablo, hablas, habla, hablamos, and hablan all look different — each ending is tied to one specific box.

This also means Spanish speakers can often drop the subject pronoun entirely, since the verb ending already tells you who's doing the action. Hablo already means "I speak" — you don't need to also say "yo."

The Box of Six isn't just for verbs, either. The same six categories come back later when you learn object pronouns (words like "me," "you," "him," "them"). Learn this box well now, and you'll reuse it constantly.

The finer points of the Box of Six

›

Everything below is optional depth — none of it is required to use the Box of Six well. Come back to this section anytime something below feels relevant.

Why is the middle-right box empty?

Remember how English uses "you" for both singular and plural? Spanish actually has a word for plural "you" — vosotros — but it's mostly used in Spain. Across Latin America, when you're talking to more than one person, you use ustedes instead, and it sits in the 3rd-person-plural box, not 2nd. Since Spanish doesn't use a separate plural-you verb form here, ustedes simply borrows the 3rd-person-plural forms. That's exactly why the vosotros box stays empty in this course.

Two ways to say "you"

Spanish has two singular "you" pronouns: tú (informal — friends, family, people your age) and usted (formal — teachers, strangers, elders, anyone you'd address respectfully). English used to have this same split — "thou" was the informal "you," while "you" was the formal, respectful version. Over time, thou disappeared, and "you" took over both jobs (part of why English's box shows the same word twice). Spanish kept both forms. One catch: tú stays in its own 2nd-person box, but usted is grammatically treated as 3rd person — its verb endings match él/ella, not tú.

What about vosotros, really?

Vosotros is the informal plural "you" used in Spain, with its own unique set of verb endings. It's mostly a Spain-only form — across Latin America, ustedes covers plural "you" in both formal and informal situations, so vosotros isn't needed here. You may run into it watching shows or movies from Spain, but you won't need it for this course.

What happened to "it"?

You may have noticed Spanish has no word for "it" anywhere in the box. That's not an oversight — Spanish just doesn't use a subject pronoun for it the way English does. Inanimate subjects typically just use the verb conjugation on its own, without a stated pronoun.

A quick word on masculine and feminine

You may have noticed nosotros/nosotras and ellos/ellas each come in two forms. Here's the basic rule: use the -as form when everyone in the group is female. Use the -os form for an all-male group — and for a mixed group, even just one male among many females. This full topic (grammatical gender, and how it extends to articles and adjectives too) gets its own dedicated unit later.

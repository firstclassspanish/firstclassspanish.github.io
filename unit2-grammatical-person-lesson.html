UNIT 2 · GRAMMATICAL PERSON

Every sentence has a subject.

A subject is the person or thing (noun) doing the action. Every subject in a sentence falls into one of six categories — once you can spot which one, everything else in Spanish grammar gets easier.

The real value of this becomes clear once we get into conjugation. Don't worry if that word is unfamiliar yet — it's an essential skill, and we'll walk you through it step by step.

💡 Why it matters

Subject pronouns are the foundation of Spanish conjugation — every verb ending you'll ever learn depends on knowing which of these six boxes a subject falls into.

Three quick definitions 🔤

Subject — the person or thing (noun) doing the action. "Elena runs." "We speak Spanish." Elena and we are the subjects.

Pronoun — a word that stands in for a name (noun). "Elena runs. She is fast." She replaces Elena.

Subject pronoun — a pronoun that replaces the subject specifically. Yo, tú, él, ella... — these are the words that fill the Box of Six you're about to learn.

Every sentence has a subject — often expressed as a subject pronoun. That's exactly what this unit teaches you to recognize and use.

What is "grammatical person"? 🧩

It answers one question: who is the subject, relative to the speaker? Three possible answers:

1st person
The speaker
Me, or me and others.
I · we
2nd person
The one spoken to
The person (or people) you're talking to.
you
3rd person
Everyone else
Anyone or anything else being talked about.
he · she · it · they
Every subject fits one of these three. That's the whole concept — the rest is just recognizing it quickly.

Meet the Box of Six 📦

These six boxes are Spanish's subject pronouns, organized by person and number. Person tells you who. Number tells you how many — singular or plural. Combine both, and you get six boxes.

Why this matters 💡

In Spanish, every verb ending changes based on which box its subject belongs to. That's why hablo (yo), hablas (tú), habla (él/ella/usted), hablamos (nosotros), and hablan (ellos/ustedes) all look different — each one is tied to a different box (grammatical person).

It also means speakers can often drop the subject pronoun entirely. Hablo already means "I speak" — no need to also say "yo."

Same six categories, later for object pronouns too ("me," "you," "him," "them"). Learn this box well now — you'll reuse it constantly.
Go Further

The finer points of the Box of Six

›

Everything below is optional depth — none of it is required to use the Box of Six well. Come back to this section anytime something below feels relevant.

Why is the middle-right box empty?

Remember how English uses "you" for both singular and plural? Spanish actually has a word for plural "you" — vosotros — but it's mostly used in Spain. Across Latin America, when you're talking to more than one person, you use ustedes instead, and it sits in the 3rd person-person-plural box, not 2nd. Since Spanish doesn't use a separate plural-you verb form here, ustedes simply borrows the 3rd-person-plural forms. That's exactly why the vosotros box stays empty in this course.

Two ways to say "you"

Spanish has two singular "you" pronouns: tú (informal — friends, family, people your age) and usted (formal — teachers, strangers, elders, anyone you'd address respectfully). English used to have this same split — "thou" was the informal "you," while "you" was the formal, respectful version. Over time, thou disappeared, and "you" took over both jobs (part of why English's box shows the same word twice). Spanish kept both forms. One catch: tú stays in its own 2nd-person box, but usted is grammatically treated as 3rd person — its verb endings match él/ella, not tú.

What about vosotros, really?

Vosotros is the informal plural "you" used in Spain, with its own unique set of verb endings. It's mostly a Spain-only form — across Latin America, ustedes covers plural "you" in both formal and informal situations, so vosotros isn't needed here. You may run into it watching shows or movies from Spain, but you won't need it for this course.

What happened to "it"?

You may have noticed Spanish has no word for "it" anywhere in the box. That's not an oversight — Spanish just doesn't use a subject pronoun for it the way English does. Inanimate subjects typically just use the verb conjugation on its own, without a stated pronoun.

A quick word on masculine and feminine

You may have noticed nosotros/nosotras and ellos/ellas each come in two forms. Here's the basic rule: use the -as form when everyone in the group is female. Use the -os form for an all-male group — and for a mixed group, even just one male among many females. This full topic (grammatical gender, and how it extends to articles and adjectives too) gets its own dedicated unit later.

UNIT 5 · NOUNS

Every noun has a gender.

You already know a noun is a person, place, thing, or idea (Unit 3). Now let's learn what Spanish nouns do that English nouns don't: every single one is either masculine or feminine — and that choice ripples through the rest of the sentence.

Key question

What makes Spanish nouns different from English ones?

🎯 What you'll learn
  • What grammatical gender means, and why it's not about the object itself
  • The common patterns for spotting masculine and feminine nouns
  • How to make any Spanish noun plural
💡 Why it matters

Gender and number are the hooks everything else hangs on — articles, adjectives, and more all have to match the noun. Get comfortable with nouns first, and agreement (coming up next) will feel like a small step, not a big one.

Two things every noun has 🏷️

In Unit 3, you learned a noun is a person, place, thing, or idea. In Spanish, every noun also carries two extra pieces of information — and you'll need both for the rest of this unit.

Gender — every Spanish noun is either masculine or feminine. Not because the object itself is "male" or "female" — it's just a grammatical category, like a label every noun is born with. El libro (the book) is masculine. La mesa (the table) is feminine.

Number — whether the noun is singular or plural, same as English. Un libro (one book) vs. dos libros (two books).

Every noun has a gender and a number — and both of them matter far more in Spanish than they do in English.

Spotting the pattern 🔍

Spanish gender can feel random at first, but most nouns actually follow a pattern based on how they end.

Usually masculine: ends in -o
el libro (the book)
el gato (the cat)
el carro (the car)
Usually feminine: ends in -a
la mesa (the table)
la casa (the house)
la ventana (the window)

This pattern gets you most of the way there — but not all the way. There are real exceptions (el problema, la mano), and plenty of nouns don't end in -o or -a at all. You'll meet more of those in the Go Further section below.

When in doubt, learn a noun together with its article — el libro, not just libro. The article is your built-in gender reminder.

Making nouns plural ➕

Spanish pluralization has three clear rules, based on how the singular noun ends.

Singular ends in... Rule Example
a vowel add -s libro → libros
a consonant add -es papel → papeles
-z change z → c, add -es lápiz → lápices
Same three rules, every time, for every regular noun. Once you've got them, you can pluralize almost anything.

Go Further

Not every noun follows the -o / -a pattern

›

The -o/-a pattern is a strong starting point, but real Spanish has plenty of nouns that don't fit it. Worth knowing about even before you've memorized them all.

Exceptions to the pattern

A handful of common -a words are actually masculine — el problema, el día, el mapa, el planeta. And a few -o words are feminine — la mano, and shortened words like la foto (from fotografía) and la moto (from motocicleta), which keep the gender of the longer word they came from. These get memorized individually.

Nouns that don't end in -o or -a

Many nouns end in other letters entirely, and their gender has to be learned along with the word — la ciudad (the city), la clase (the class). Endings like -e and -en are especially unpredictable: la fuente (the fountain) and el puente (the bridge) look almost identical but take opposite genders — and so do el examen (the exam) and la imagen (the image).

Nouns for people can change with gender

Words describing people often have a masculine and feminine form tied to the person's own gender — el amigo / la amiga, el maestro / la maestra. Same pattern, but describing a real person instead of an object.

Why some feminine words use "el"

Words like el agua (water) and el águila (eagle) look masculine because of "el" — but they're actually feminine. Spanish swaps la for el right before a feminine word that starts with a stressed "a" sound, purely to make it easier to say — la agua would force two "a" sounds together back to back. The word itself never changes gender: el agua fría, not "el agua frío." This only happens in the singular — in the plural it's las aguas, because las ends in a consonant (s), so the awkward vowel collision never happens in the first place. You'll see this "el" trick and more tricky-gender words like this in a future unit built just for cases like these.
